Basal Cell Carcinoma

Understanding Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC): Causes, Symptoms

What is Basal Cell Carcinoma?

Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC) is the most common type of skin cancer, arising from the basal cells in the outer layer of the skin (epidermis). BCC typically develops in areas exposed to the sun, such as the face, neck, and ears, but it can occur anywhere on the body. Although BCC rarely spreads to other parts of the body (metastasizes), it can grow locally and invade surrounding tissues if left untreated.

Causes of Basal Cell Carcinoma

BCC is primarily caused by long-term exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun or artificial sources like tanning beds. Other factors that may increase the risk of developing BCC include:

  • Skin Type: Individuals with fair skin, light hair, and light eyes are at a higher risk.
  • Age: The risk increases with age, as skin becomes more susceptible to damage over time.
  • Previous Skin Cancer: Having a history of nonmelanoma skin cancers increases the likelihood of developing BCC.
  • Family History: A family history of skin cancer can elevate the risk.
  • Weakened Immune System: Individuals with weakened immune systems, such as organ transplant recipients or those with certain diseases, are more vulnerable.
  • Exposure to Chemicals: Prolonged exposure to certain chemicals, like arsenic or industrial compounds, can increase the risk.

Symptoms of Basal Cell Carcinoma

BCC can manifest in several forms, and its appearance can vary. Common signs and symptoms include:

  • Pearly or Waxy Bump: Often appears as a small, shiny bump that resembles a pearl.
  • Flat, Flesh-Colored Lesion: A flat, scaly area that may be similar in color to the surrounding skin.
  • Red or Brown Patches: These can be rough or scaly and may bleed or crust over.
  • Open Sores: Sores that do not heal or that heal and then return can be a sign of BCC.
  • Scar-Like Area: A flat, scar-like area that appears white, yellow, or waxy.

It is essential to monitor any changes in your skin and consult a healthcare provider if you notice any unusual growths or changes in existing moles or spots.

Diagnosis of Basal Cell Carcinoma

If BCC is suspected, a dermatologist will perform a thorough skin examination and may conduct a biopsy, where a small sample of the suspicious area is removed and examined under a microscope. This helps confirm the diagnosis and determine the appropriate treatment.
