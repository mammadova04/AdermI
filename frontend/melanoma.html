Melanoma

Understanding Melanoma: Causes, Symptoms

What is Melanoma?

Melanoma is a serious form of skin cancer that develops from melanocytes, the cells responsible for producing the pigment melanin, which gives skin its color. Although melanoma is less common than other skin cancers, it is more aggressive and has a higher potential to spread to other parts of the body (metastasize). Early detection and treatment are crucial for improving outcomes.

Symptoms of Melanoma

Melanoma can appear as a new mole or changes in an existing mole. Key signs to watch for include:

  • Asymmetry: One half of the mole or spot does not match the other half in size, shape, or color.
  • Irregular Borders: Melanomas often have jagged, notched, or blurred edges.
  • Varied Color: The color may vary from one area to another and can include shades of brown, black, tan, red, white, or blue.
  • Diameter: Melanomas are usually larger than 6 millimeters (about the size of a pencil eraser), but they can be smaller when detected.
  • Evolving: Any change in size, shape, color, or elevation of a mole, or any new symptom such as bleeding, itching, or crusting, should be examined by a healthcare professional.

Risk Factors for Melanoma

While anyone can develop melanoma, certain factors increase the risk:

  • UV Exposure: Prolonged exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun or tanning beds is a significant risk factor.
  • Fair Skin: People with light skin, freckles, and light-colored eyes have a higher risk.
  • Family History: A family history of melanoma or other skin cancers increases susceptibility.
  • Personal History: Individuals with a personal history of melanoma or other skin cancers are at greater risk.
  • Moles: Having many moles (more than 50) or atypical moles (dysplastic nevi) can increase risk.
  • Immune Suppression: Individuals with weakened immune systems, such as organ transplant recipients or those with certain autoimmune diseases, are more vulnerable.

Diagnosis of Melanoma

If melanoma is suspected, a dermatologist will perform a thorough examination of the skin and may conduct a biopsy to remove a portion of the mole or spot for laboratory analysis. The biopsy results will confirm the diagnosis and help determine the stage of the cancer, which is crucial for planning treatment.
