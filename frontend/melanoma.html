Melanoma

Understanding Melanoma: Causes, Symptoms, and Risk Factors

What is Melanoma?

Melanoma is a serious form of skin cancer that develops from melanocytes, the cells responsible for producing the pigment melanin. It is less common but more aggressive than other skin cancers, with a higher potential to spread to other parts of the body (metastasize). Early detection and treatment are crucial for improving outcomes.

Symptoms of Melanoma

Melanoma can appear as a new mole or changes in an existing mole. Key signs include:

  • Asymmetry: One half of the mole does not match the other.
  • Irregular Borders: Jagged, notched, or blurred edges.
  • Varied Color: Multiple colors like brown, black, tan, or even red and blue.
  • Diameter: Larger than 6mm (pencil eraser size), but can be smaller when detected early.
  • Evolving: Changes in size, shape, or symptoms like bleeding, itching, or crusting.

Risk Factors for Melanoma

While anyone can develop melanoma, certain factors increase the risk:

  • UV Exposure: Prolonged exposure to UV radiation from the sun or tanning beds.
  • Fair Skin: People with lighter skin, freckles, and light-colored eyes are at higher risk.
  • Family History: Having a family history of melanoma increases susceptibility.
  • Moles: Having more than 50 moles or atypical moles (dysplastic nevi).
  • Immune Suppression: Weak immune systems increase vulnerability.

Diagnosis of Melanoma

If melanoma is suspected, a dermatologist will perform a skin examination and may conduct a biopsy to confirm the diagnosis. The biopsy helps determine the cancer's stage, which is crucial for planning treatment.
